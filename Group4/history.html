Reggae music emerged in the late 1960s in Jamaica, rooted in the island's rich musical traditions of ska and rocksteady. Known for its signature offbeat rhythms and socially conscious lyrics, reggae quickly became a voice for the oppressed, addressing themes of social justice, spirituality, and resistance. The genre gained international attention in the 1970s, spearheaded by iconic artists like Bob Marley, Peter Tosh, and Jimmy Cliff, who brought reggae's message of unity and resilience to the global stage. Over time, reggae influenced and was influenced by other genres, blending with rock, hip-hop, and electronic music to create new sounds while maintaining its distinctive identity. Today, reggae remains a powerful cultural force, celebrated for its enduring impact on music, activism, and the fight for equality. This history encapsulates the genre's evolution from its humble beginnings in Kingston to its recognition as a global symbol of hope and empowerment.
